South Dakota needed to modernize how citizens interacted with state government services. We delivered a ServiceNow-powered platform with custom UX, intuitive information architecture, single sign-on, and mobile-first case management.

Challenge

A fragmented ecosystem designed around government org charts — not human needs.

Most citizen services existed in silos across separate agency websites. Citizens had to know which department managed a specific service before they could even begin. If they guessed wrong, they started over. There was no centralized search, no unified login, no shared navigation structure — and no way to track the status of anything once submitted.

"People with health conditions and single parents working two jobs can't wait for assistance. They don't have the time to navigate a complex site, wait on hold, or travel to government offices."

— Heather Perry, Deputy Commissioner, State of South Dakota

Research

Citizens organize government services around life events — not agency names.

That was the central finding from card sorting sessions with 32 South Dakota residents across four demographic groups. When asked to group services, participants consistently reached for categories like "starting a business," "renewing a license," or "getting assistance" — not "Department of Labor" or "Bureau of Finance and Management."

Tree testing validated the final taxonomy before any visual design was completed. 89% first-click accuracy across 32 participants. Citizens shouldn't need to understand how government is organized to access government services.

Reflection

The hardest part wasn't the design. It was the alignment.

Seventeen agencies, four bureaus, years of accumulated process, and staff who have been doing things a specific way for a long time. Getting everyone to a shared foundation required as much facilitation as it did design.

The research made the case. Citizens weren't struggling because they weren't trying hard enough. They were struggling because the system had never been designed with them as the starting point. A portal that works for everyone starts by asking what everyone actually needs — before a single wireframe exists.
