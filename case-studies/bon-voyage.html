Bon Voyage

The travel planning market was saturated. Every major platform does one thing well and nothing else. Bon Voyage was designed to be the missing product: an itinerary-first travel platform built around how budget-conscious travelers actually plan trips.

Certification Flatiron School Solo

Role

Solo UX Designer

Timeline

6 Weeks

Type

Certification Project

Challenge

The market was missing it. The research confirmed it.

Users struggled to find affordable hidden gems and local experiences. Multiple apps were required just to organize a single trip. Spreadsheets served as itineraries. Blogs stood in for local guides.

The gap wasn't supply. It was structure. No platform existed that combined discovery, planning, social sharing, and local guidance in a single experience built around the traveler's budget and itinerary — not the platform's revenue model.

Research

Budget consciousness is universal. The market has no all-inclusive platform.

Seven competitors analyzed: TripAdvisor, Kayak, Airbnb, TripIt, Yelp, Lonely Planet, and Facebook Groups. The pattern was consistent — strong in one dimension, weak everywhere else.

User interviews revealed five consistent findings. Users are budget-conscious and frugal. All users rely on online resources to plan but were consistently unsatisfied with the outcomes. Social media, curated lists, and itineraries are the primary influencers for trip planning — not search engines, not booking platforms.

This shaped the persona. Emma Harris is a 27-year-old technical recruiter from Brooklyn — energetic, social, resourceful, and budget-conscious. She wants authentic local experiences, plans trips months in advance, and is consistently frustrated by the inability to organize local excursions ahead of time.

The frugal traveler needs to budget and research a trip so that they can create an organized itinerary to feel comfortable in their travels.

Design Process

Six weeks. One clear direction.

Mid-fidelity wireframes built out five task flows: onboarding with interest selection, creating a new trip, reserving an activity, managing a profile, and adding trip categories.

Two style tiles were tested. The first was sophisticated and airy — users felt it was too feminine. The second was modeled after a handwritten travel journal — users felt it matched the feeling of creating a personalized itinerary. The second direction became the high-fidelity foundation.

Reflection

The product that came out was better than the one that went in.

Creating a new product from scratch is time-consuming and labor-intensive in a way that's hard to anticipate until you're in it. This project reinforced the value of iteration — and the value of stepping away. Fresh eyes on your own work catch things that hours of staring at it won't.

The final design looked significantly different from the original vision. That's not a failure. That's the process working correctly. User input consistently outperformed competitive research and personal assumptions.
