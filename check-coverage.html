Check Coverage

Check Coverage started as a compliance obligation. It became something members actually relied on. That shift didn't happen in a single sprint — it happened over three years of research, analytics, and design decisions that kept moving the product closer to how members actually think.

Healthcare Express Scripts

Role

UX Design Advisor

Timeline

2022 – 2025

Platform

Responsive · Native

Challenge

The problem looked simple at first.

A tool existed, members weren't using it, and the language was hard to understand. But the deeper issue wasn't terminology.

Members didn't struggle with Check Coverage because the words were wrong. They struggled because the entire experience was organized around how benefits work internally — not around what members were actually trying to do.

Availability of information did not equal clarity or confidence. And without confidence, members called instead of clicked.

Objectives

From disclosure to decision support.

Move the experience from disclosure-focused transparency toward something genuinely useful — clear, explainable, and designed around real decisions. Use research and analytics not as validation, but as a continuous feedback loop that could keep the product honest over time.

Starting Point

Success was defined by whether information was present.

When Check Coverage launched in 2022, success was defined by whether information was present and accurate. Not whether anyone could understand it. Not whether it helped anyone make a decision.

The experience reflected that priority. Information was structured around benefit logic. Terminology came from insurance, not from everyday language. There was no meaningful support for comparison or for understanding why a price looked the way it did.

The tool met its regulatory requirements. Early research made clear that was the ceiling, not the floor.

What the Research Kept Telling Us

Research ran continuously from 2022 through 2025.

Price comes first.

Members orient to cost immediately, then use coverage details to explain why that number looks the way it does. Coverage without a price feels incomplete. A price without an explanation feels untrustworthy.

Language shapes confidence more than precision.

Members responded better to "not covered" than to "outside plan." Better to "in network" than to "recommended pharmacy." Getting this right wasn't about simplification — it was about meeting members where their mental model already was.

Comparison is the job.

Members didn't want to be guided through a decision funnel. They wanted to scan options, evaluate tradeoffs, and choose. Every time we watched members use the tool, they bypassed category framing and went straight to prices.

Confidence breaks at transitions.

Confusion spiked at specific moments — a new prescription, a brand switching to generic, a deductible resetting, a pharmacy change. These were the moments that drove calls. The design goal shifted from reducing confusion generally to preventing surprise specifically.

The window is narrow.

Check Coverage is most useful after a medication is discussed and before a member reaches the pharmacy counter. That window shaped every entry point, every piece of messaging, and every information hierarchy decision.

Where the Design Went Wrong First

Translating research into design isn't linear.

The clearest example was an interstitial screen placed mid-flow — a confirmation step that asked members to verify their intent before showing results. It seemed reasonable. It was a friction point we thought would protect the experience quality downstream.

Analytics told a different story. Drop-off concentrated exactly there. Members who cleared the interstitial continued at high rates. Members who didn't, left. The step wasn't protecting anyone — it was standing between members and the information they came for.

Removing it wasn't the insight. The insight was that the entire flow had been organized around our assumptions about what members needed to do, rather than what they were actually trying to accomplish.

The Design Principles That Held

Less rules, more shared language.

01

Lead with cost, support with explanation.

Price should be visible early. Members should not have to hunt for the number that matters most.

02

Use familiar language, then explain nuance.

Start with words members already know. Earn the right to go deeper.

03

Treat comparison as the primary experience.

Comparison is not a feature — it is the job. The interface should make side-by-side evaluation the default.

04

Make change explicit.

When something differs from what a member expected, name it. Silence around change is the source of most confusion.

05

Design for confidence, not just task completion.

A member who completes the flow but doesn't understand what they decided hasn't been served.

Outcomes

Reflection

The most useful thing wasn't any single design decision. It was the continuity.

Most products get redesigned once and then drift. Check Coverage got sustained attention — research that compounded, analytics that built on itself, design decisions that were made with the context of what came before.

The strategic reframe — from disclosure to decision support, from benefit logic to shopping behavior — was the pivot that made everything else possible. But the pivot only worked because there was enough accumulated understanding to make it with confidence.

Designing in regulated spaces doesn't mean designing defensively. It means earning the right, over time, to make the experience simpler than the system behind it.
