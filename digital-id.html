Digital ID

This wasn't a redesign. It was a transformation — from a physical artifact members received in the mail to a digital experience they carry in their pocket.

Role

UX Strategy Lead

Platform

Responsive · Native

Scope

Enterprise · Multi-LOB

Challenge

The digital version existed. It just wasn't useful.

For years, members got a physical ID card in the mail. That card was the artifact of record — the thing they pulled out at the pharmacy counter, handed to a provider, or tucked into their wallet for safekeeping.

The digital version existed, technically. But it was a single-sided SVG rendering with a handful of data points — not a complete representation of what was actually on the card. Members couldn't copy from it, share it meaningfully, or add it to their phone's wallet. It lived several levels deep inside an Account menu with no dedicated entry point.

And most importantly, members were still getting physical cards in the mail, so there was no reason to look for the digital one. Low traffic, minimal engagement, almost no repeat usage. The digital experience wasn't failing because it was broken. It was failing because it had never been given a real reason to exist.

Original: partial SVG, buried in Account menu

The Real Problem

The instinct was to improve the card. That would have been the wrong move.

Because the card wasn't the problem. The program was.

As long as physical cards kept arriving in the mail, digital would always be the backup. The transformation only became possible when the organization committed to ending the physical card program entirely. No more welcome kits in the mail. No more cards.

Members would receive a digital welcome kit instead — and that kit would serve as an entry point into web registration, putting the ID card at the beginning of the member relationship rather than buried somewhere inside it.

Digitizing a physical artifact is not transformation. Designing for real-world behavior is.

The Reframe

When do people actually need their ID card?

Not when do they log in and look for it. When does the real-world need arise?

We mapped six moments that changed the design direction entirely. The card wasn't a page to improve — it was an asset that needed to be in the right place at the right time.

Provider check-in

Presenting at a provider's office — needs to be instantly visible and trustworthy.

Pharmacy counter

Needs to display the right data, fast, with the ability to share or show without hunting.

Sharing with a dependent

Managing household coverage — person-picker needs to be fast and intuitive.

Offline access

Downloading for places where connectivity isn't guaranteed.

Digital wallet

Adding to Apple Wallet or Google Wallet — where members already keep their important cards.

Pre-effective access

Accessing coverage information before benefits are even effective yet.

Zooming Out First

Before any design work began, a more fundamental question.

What actually exists across the ecosystem today? Starting with the ESI experience and improving it in isolation would have meant building on a partial picture — and potentially recreating the same fragmentation accumulated across lines of business over time.

A cross-ecosystem frequency exercise mapped every element, feature, and interaction pattern appearing across ID card experiences organization-wide. Each component — card image, side switcher, person picker, wallet integration, share and download, zoom view, card details, contact information, covered plans, glossary — was plotted against every line of business to identify what was universal, what was inconsistent, and what was missing entirely.

Five Decisions That Changed the Experience

Not incremental improvements. Structural shifts.

01

Restore visual trust.

Replaced the partial SVG rendering with actual card images — front and back — showing the complete card as it exists. Members who saw a card that matched their mental model trusted it immediately.

02

Surface structured data.

Made the card data available as readable, copyable text — not just embedded in an image. Members who needed their RxBIN at the pharmacy counter no longer had to zoom into a picture and squint.

03

Build for real-world utility.

Added wallet integration, share sheet, download, and print — the actions members actually needed to take, not just a static display.

04

Make it findable.

Moved the entry point out of the Account menu and into a dedicated location in the navigation — a change that drove the majority of usage to the new location almost immediately post-launch.

05

Design for the household.

Built a person-picker that made it fast to switch between subscriber and dependent cards — because household management was real and frequent, representing a meaningful share of total sessions.

Working with AI as a Design Partner

Not to demonstrate. To explore.

There was one interaction problem that kept stopping forward momentum: how do you support a swipe gesture for moving between cards while also keeping a tap accessible for members who can't rely on gesture navigation?

In the past, answering it for stakeholders meant hours of prototyping an interaction that might get revised the next day anyway. This time, Figma Make became a thinking tool.

Rather than prototyping to demonstrate, the goal was to explore — to throw out multiple approaches fast enough to have a real conversation about tradeoffs. Make generated mobile explorations showing different ways to handle the front-to-back card swap and the swipe-between-multiple-cards interaction for ESI use cases. Not final designs. Starting points that could be reacted to, discarded, or pushed further.

The tool didn't make the design decisions. It made the design decisions faster to reach.

What the Analytics Confirmed

Usage patterns told a consistent story.

The visual card is the dominant mental model.

When a text-based alternative view was introduced, an estimated 1% or fewer of members engaged with it. Of those, roughly half switched back to the image view. The card image is not a preference. It is the expectation.

Saving is the primary job.

Wallet add, share sheet, download, and print consistently drove the highest estimated interaction volume. These aren't supporting features — they are the reason members come to the experience.

Household management is real and frequent.

Person-picker interactions — switching between a subscriber's card and a dependent's — represented a meaningful share of total sessions. Members routinely manage cards for their whole household.

Outreach moves behavior.

Email campaigns directed at the ID card experience produced estimated session spikes with strong click-through rates. Members weren't avoiding the feature. They weren't aware of it.

Outcomes

Traffic increase

Dedicated nav entry point drove majority of usage to new location almost immediately post-launch.

Scalable identity foundation

Reflection

The card got better. The program got leaner. The work that came after is the part that will last.

The central insight wasn't about cards. It was about what transformation actually requires.

It's not enough to build a better digital experience if the physical one still exists. It's not enough to redesign a feature if the entry point remains invisible. And it's not enough to ship something once if the organization isn't aligned around a shared foundation going forward.

True digital transformation meant ending the physical program, not just improving the digital one. It meant pairing product work with adoption strategy. It meant building something rigorous enough to scale — so that the next line of business doesn't start from scratch, and the member experience doesn't fragment further every time a new implementation gets built.
